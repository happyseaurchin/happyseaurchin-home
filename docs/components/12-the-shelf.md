# 12 — The Shelf

**Category:** Interface Layer  
**Products:** Xstream, Onen

---

## Description

The Shelf operates as a commitment gradient that governs how information moves from private thought to shared reality. It defines three states: **Vapor** (thinking aloud, ephemeral, high revision risk), **Liquid** (committed intention, visible to others, stable), and **Solid** (synthesized, integrated into shared narrative, nearly immutable). This three-state model creates a strategic tension between initiative (who moves text from vapor to liquid) and information (who controls what becomes solid). The Shelf is not a document storage system; it is a coordination mechanism that makes commitment visible and reversible at each stage.

---

## Standalone Use

Any multi-participant coordination effort could use the Shelf pattern: a group writing project, a design critique, a planning session, or a distributed research team. Instead of documents accumulating in shared folders with unclear versions, the Shelf makes visible which ideas are still tentative, which are committed proposals, and which have been synthesized into group consensus. Moving something from vapor to liquid requires acknowledgment (not approval, but notice). Moving to solid requires integration into the broader narrative or record.

---

## How It Works

**Vapor state**: Text submitted to vapor is ephemeral. It persists for the duration of a session but is not recorded in the persistent narrative. This is safe space for rough thinking, questions, and exploration. Vapor is visible to all participants but carries an implicit expectation: "I might change my mind."

**Liquid state**: Commitment to liquid is a deliberate act. The writer is saying "I stand by this." The text becomes visible not just to present participants but is recorded for future reference. Liquid can still be edited or withdrawn, but the edit is tracked. This is where proposals live, where questions become recorded inquiries, where ideas are proposed for group consideration.

**Solid state**: Solid text is integrated into the compiled narrative or record. It is the "what happened" or "what we agreed" version of events. Solid requires synthesis — not every liquid statement becomes solid, but the solid state should account for what made it into the narrative. Solid can be amended (for factual correction) but amendments are noted.

**The initiative tension**: In Xstream, any participant can move their own text from vapor to liquid. Only a designated synthesizer (or consensus mechanism) can move text to solid. This creates productive tension: leaders must notice and integrate what the group cares about, not dictate what matters. In Onen, characters (players and NPCs) move their own action-packages to liquid; the Medium-LLM (game narrator) selects what becomes solid.

**Compilation rhythm**: The Shelf operates on pscale timing. Vapor may be reviewed and revised at high frequency (minute-scale). Liquid submissions are processed on the compilation rhythm (approximately 1-minute intervals in Xstream, variable in Onen). Solid integration happens at longer intervals or at narrative closure points.
